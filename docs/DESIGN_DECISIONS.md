# Introduction

## Project Overview
The Intercultural Communication Workshop was developed as an interactive web-based learning experience designed to help learners build intercultural competence through reflection, practice, and application rather than memorization. 
This workshop draws on established research and practice in intercultural communication and adult learning while also incorporating insights gained through years of teaching international students and facilitating intercultural learning experiences. 
The workshop was originally developed from a series of face-to-face lessons taught to groups of multicultural international students studying both in the US and abroad. During this project, those lessons were redesigned into a self-paced online learning experience while preserving the reflection, and experiential nature of the original curriculum. In future designs the discussion aspect can be added. 
Rather than focusing on factual knowledge about cultures, the workshop emphasizes developing habits and providing tools to navigate intercultural experiences. This is to make the program more useful to a wider audience by focusing on reusable skills and mindsets.
Throughout the workshop, learners repeatedly return to one overarching question: How does this activity help me become more aware of my own assumptions and make more thoughtful intercultural decisions? While each learning outcome is important, this guiding question represents the central purpose of the workshop.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this workshop, learners will be able to:
### 1. Develop Cultural Awareness
- Explain culture as a dynamic system of visible and invisible influences.
- Recognize how culture influences the way people perceive and interpret the world.
- Identify stereotypes, assumptions, and personal biases that influence understanding.
### 2. Develop Tolerance of Ambiguity
- Recognize ambiguous situations and respond thoughtfully before reaching conclusions.
- Practice intercultural reflection to better understand unfamiliar cultural situations.
- Use the DAEA framework to distinguish observations, interpretations, and emotional responses.
- Critically reflect on unfamiliar situations from multiple perspectives before making judgments.
### 3. Navigate Future Intercultural Experiences
- Analyze intercultural situations from multiple perspectives.
- Respond thoughtfully rather than reacting to first impressions.
- Create a personal action plan for approaching future intercultural experiences with curiosity, openness, and reflection.

## The Learning Problem
A central design decision for this workshop was to prioritize a practical decision-making framework over teaching a large number of cultural theories or dimensions. While models such as high- and low-context communication or individualism and collectivism can provide valuable insights, learners often encounter unfamiliar situations before they have mastered these concepts. This workshop therefore introduces a repeatable framework that learners can apply immediately while continuing to develop their intercultural knowledge. As learners encounter new cultural ideas throughout the workshop and beyond, they can use this same framework to observe, reflect, gather information, and adapt their understanding. 
For this we leaned heavily into the concept of Tolerance of Ambiguity. Helping learners develop the skills to navigate through various intercultural situations more smoothly. This is coupled with an emphasis of using Critical Reflection to not only tease apart your experience but also come up with a plan of action if you encounter this situation again. This includes suggestions for speaking with a cultural informant if you have one, or researching the topic if you don’t.

## Supporting Both Learners and Reviewers
One design challenge was balancing the needs of learners with the expectations of instructional design professionals. While this workshop is fully functional as a learning experience (and I hope many people use it that way), this implementation also serves as a portfolio project intended to demonstrate my instructional design process to hiring managers and other instructional design professionals. Rather than presenting learning outcomes before every activity, the workshop uses learner-centered debriefs that explain how each module builds practical skills. For learners, this answers the question, "Why did I do this activity?" in accessible language. Behind the scenes, each debrief is mapped to the workshop's formal learning outcomes through module metadata, demonstrating constructive alignment without overwhelming learners with instructional terminology. Learners don’t care what objective they fulfilled in the lesson, but they really do want to know why they did it.

...

## Learning Philosophy
The workshop is built around a simple instructional philosophy:
Learn > Practice > Reflect > Apply
Learners first encounter a concept. They then practice using that concept through interactive activities. After practicing, they reflect on their thinking. Finally, they identify how they can apply the concept in future real-world situations. The workshop intentionally avoids treating reflection as an assessment. Instead, reflection is used to help learners prepare for future experiences.
This is all built on the philosophy that much of your learning comes through reflecting on experiences. You can experience something but may not learn from it unless you reflect on it. Thus this training focuses on using reflections of both past experiences and on hypotheticals to help learners get into the practice of using reflections to learn and come up with strategies for the future.

# Instructional Design Decisions

## Focus on Competencies
Instead of teaching learners "how other cultures behave," the workshop emphasizes competencies such as:
> Critical reflection
> Perspective taking
> Tolerance of ambiguity
> Curiosity
> Delaying judgment
These competencies remain useful regardless of the cultural context.

...

## Reflection Throughout the Course
Reflection was intentionally distributed throughout the workshop instead of appearing only at the end.
This approach allows learners to revisit their thinking after each major concept and reinforces metacognition as an ongoing process.
Each reflection is saved automatically and revisited during the final Reflection Summary. In this summary the prompts and reflections are then compared to the course objectives showing the learner what skills they practiced in the course.
...

## Constructive Alignment
Every activity is intentionally aligned with one or more learning objectives.
Rather than hiding this alignment from learners, the Reflection Summary explicitly shows:
> why each reflection was included,
> which competencies it helped learners practice,
> and how those activities connect to the overall goals of the workshop.
This design helps learners understand the purpose behind each activity rather than viewing reflections as isolated exercises.
The materials, exercises, and reflections were all made with the learning objectives in mind. 

...

## Practice Rather Than Mastery
One of the most important design decisions was avoiding language that implies mastery.
The workshop does not claim learners become interculturally competent after completing a short course. True mastery comes through experience, practice, and reflection. Instead, the workshop provides learners with the foundational tools and habits they can continue developing through future intercultural experiences. 
Instead, learners practice the habits associated with intercultural competence in a safe learning environment. 
The final module emphasizes that continued growth occurs through real-world experience followed by thoughtful reflection.

# Learning Activities

## Instructional Design Note
The learning activities were intentionally sequenced so that each module builds on the knowledge and skills developed in the previous module. Learners first develop conceptual understanding, then practice structured reflection, and finally apply these skills to increasingly complex intercultural scenarios. This progression follows the workshop's three learning outcomes and gradually shifts responsibility from guided exploration to independent application.

...

## Cultural Iceberg
This section is meant to help learners come to a definition of culture for themselves. Rather than beginning with a textbook definition, learners first construct their own definition of culture. This serves as a personal self-assessment encouraging learners to reflect on their existing understanding of culture before encountering new ideas. Throughout the module learners get to compare their definition with an academic definition and gradually refine their understanding through reflection, discussion, and the Cultural Iceberg activity. The Cultural Iceberg model helps learners recognize that many of the factors influencing communication, values, and behavior are not immediately visible. The visual aspect of the cultural iceberg metaphor helps learners grasp the visible/invisible culture distinction which tends to be more effective than a lengthy academic explanation. This also encourages learners to move beyond surface-level observations when interpreting cultural differences. Once learners recognize the visible and invisible dimensions of culture, the module expands to explore how culture influences everyday decision-making and shapes the way people perceive and interpret the world around them. Learners are encouraged to recognize that different cultural perspectives can lead to different interpretations of the same event without assuming that one interpretation is inherently correct.  Learners conclude the module by revising their original definition of culture, finishing with a reflection on how their understanding has evolved through the learning activities. 
Rather than asking learners to memorize a definition of culture, the module uses reflection, examples, and questioning to guide learners to construct their own definition of culture. More importantly, learners begin recognizing that different cultural perspectives can lead to different interpretations of the same situation. Throughout the workshop, they learn to examine those interpretations through reflection rather than assuming their first impressions are correct. 
As the first module, this lesson establishes the conceptual foundation for the remainder of the workshop. Before learners can critically reflect on intercultural experiences, they must first recognize that culture influences perception and that different interpretations can exist simultaneously. These ideas are revisited and expanded throughout the remaining modules. It also introduces the concept of learning through reflecting that is used throughout the program. Finally by creating a common understanding of culture and how it shapes perception, this module prepares learners to examine stereotypes and assumptions in following lessons.

## Stereotypes
Building on the previous module, learners next examine the role stereotypes and assumptions play in shaping intercultural interactions. Rather than asking learners to completely eliminate stereotypes, which is unrealistic, the module emphasizes recognizing when stereotypes and assumptions influence interpretation. Learners are introduced to the importance of recognizing and examining stereotypes with the intention of finding more information to update their beliefs. This is practiced through a series of short reflections on common stereotypes about their own culture to highlight that they represent incomplete information. Beginning with stereotypes about learners' own culture encourages self-reflection in a psychologically safe way while demonstrating that stereotypes are often based on partial truths and incomplete information.  This is then reinforced through some exercises where they have to separate observations from interpretations, something that will be practiced in depth in later lessons. Learners are introduced to practical strategies for reducing the influence of assumptions, including consulting cultural informants, researching cultural norms, and reflecting on new experiences. 

Expectations based on inaccurate stereotypes can influence how people interpret unfamiliar situations before they have sufficient information. By helping learners recognize these expectations, the module encourages them to delay judgment and remain open to alternative explanations. The initial reflection helps learners recognize how some assumptions can be incorrect by having learners criticize assumptions about their own culture. It goes a step further by encouraging learners to seek out more accurate information to update their beliefs. Rather than simply attempting to avoid stereotypes, learners practice actively testing and refining their assumptions as they encounter new information. By recognizing that initial assumptions are often incomplete, learners begin developing the habit of questioning their first impressions and remaining open to new evidence. 

This module directly supports identifying stereotypes, assumptions, and personal biases. Recognizing the influence of assumptions prepares learners for the following module, where they practice remaining open when information is incomplete rather than rushing to conclusions. Rather than encouraging learners to suppress assumptions entirely, the module promotes curiosity by introducing the concept of a cultural informant. Learners practice seeking additional perspectives and treating unfamiliar situations as opportunities to learn rather than evidence that their first impressions are correct.

## Tolerance of Ambiguity
Intercultural experiences often place learners in situations where they do not yet understand what is happening. This module introduces a practical framework for responding thoughtfully when cultural expectations, language, or social norms are unfamiliar.Learners practice a strategy of assessing safety, delaying judgment, observing with curiosity, gathering additional information, and finally reflecting to learn from the experience. A key design decision in this module is helping learners distinguish between uncertainty and genuine risk. Feeling uncomfortable in an unfamiliar situation does not necessarily mean the situation is unsafe. Openness to new experiences should never come at the expense of personal safety. Once safety has been established, they practice delaying judgment, accepting temporary uncertainty, and approaching unfamiliar experiences with curiosity while gathering additional information. The emphasis is not on becoming comfortable with uncertainty itself, but on developing thoughtful responses when complete information is unavailable. Over time, however, this approach often helps learners become more comfortable navigating uncertainty. The final idea is that learners are encouraged to recognize that discomfort is often a natural part of intercultural learning rather than evidence that something is wrong.
Although the module is brief, it introduces the mental framework that learners repeatedly apply throughout the remainder of the workshop. This module marks the transition from understanding intercultural concepts to practicing intercultural behaviors. Where the previous modules focus on understanding how culture and assumptions influence perception, this module shifts attention toward learners' responses. Rather than reacting automatically, learners practice pausing, observing, and reflecting before deciding how to interpret unfamiliar situations. From this point onward, learners begin applying those ideas through increasingly complex reflective activities and critical incidents.  
This module serves as the turning point of the workshop. The first two modules help learners understand how culture and assumptions influence perception. From this point forward, learners begin practicing how to respond when they encounter unfamiliar situations. This module serves as a transition from the “what” that is presented in the first two modules to the “how” that should be analyzed and practiced in the rest of the workshop. Rather than presenting a one-time strategy, the module introduces a repeatable decision-making process that learners continue practicing throughout the remainder of the workshop.

## Critical Reflection and DAEA
DAEA provides learners with a structured framework for slowing down their thinking and examining unfamiliar situations before reaching conclusions. Rather than reacting immediately to unfamiliar situations, learners separate observations, interpretations, and emotional responses before considering future actions. Learners then apply DAEA to an intentionally incomplete photograph before viewing the full context, illustrating how limited information can produce inaccurate conclusions. Presenting only part of the image intentionally creates ambiguity, allowing learners to experience firsthand how easily incomplete information can lead to inaccurate interpretations. This creates a meaningful context for introducing the DAEA framework rather than simply explaining it theoretically. The module concludes by extending reflection into critical reflection. Rather than ending with analysis alone, learners consider how the experience should influence future decisions and behaviors. Reflection therefore becomes a tool for improving future intercultural interactions rather than simply explaining past events.  
DAEA was chosen as opposed to several other models, for example DIE (another term for DAE), which I was trained with, because it is explicitly action-oriented. While other reflective techniques are useful, for an online self-paced workshop like this I wanted to emphasize that reflection should be done with a goal in mind. The rest of the training has a lot of reflections and critical incidents for learners to parse through, DAEA acts as a method to approach them. Building on the previous module, DAEA gives learners a practical method for using the pause created by tolerance of ambiguity. Rather than reacting immediately, learners learn how to examine observations, interpretations, emotions, and future actions in a structured way. Rather than relying solely on learners' previous intercultural experiences, the workshop uses carefully designed scenarios and critical incidents to ensure every learner practices the same reflective process. This creates a shared learning experience while providing opportunities to apply the framework before using it independently in real situations. 
Building on the mindset introduced in the previous module, DAEA provides learners with a structured process for using the pause created by tolerance of ambiguity. Rather than allowing emotional reactions to drive interpretation, learners practice examining observations, interpretations, emotions, and future actions before deciding how to respond. While tolerance of ambiguity helps learners use critical reflection, DAEA helps expand people’s tolerance of ambiguity. By helping learners navigate future intercultural experiences DAEA gives them a tool to expand their ability to handle unknown situations. This gets learners to also see how they might not have all of the information in an experience and, thus, practice tolerance of ambiguity to navigate the experiences.

## Prague Critical Incident
This is the first of four original critical incidents presented in the original workshop. This situation is an authentic intercultural incident experienced during my English language teacher training in Prague. Using one of my own intercultural experiences allowed me to accurately recreate not only the events themselves, but also the uncertainty, assumptions, and emotional responses I experienced throughout the incident, giving learners a deeper example to work with. The incident was turned into a comic to help learners visualize the interaction. Unlike presenting the complete incident at once, the comic format intentionally reveals information in stages. Learners repeatedly interpret the situation using only the information currently available, mirroring how real intercultural misunderstandings unfold. This allows them to experience the uncertainty, assumptions, and revisions that characterize authentic intercultural interactions. Visual changes in the background also reinforce that new contextual information becomes available over time, reminding learners that interpretations should evolve as additional evidence is gathered. 
This incident provides learners with their first authentic critical incident to analyze using the framework developed throughout the workshop. Experiencing the incident from the perspective of someone navigating an unfamiliar culture encourages learners to empathize with the uncertainty and emotional responses that often accompany intercultural interactions. This module serves as the first authentic application of the concepts introduced earlier in the workshop. Rather than learning about culture in isolation, learners integrate cultural awareness, assumption checking, tolerance of ambiguity, and DAEA while working through a realistic intercultural misunderstanding. 
With the Prague critical incident, learners are given an opportunity to build on all of their concepts of cultures being different, as well as tolerance of ambiguity and DAEA in a real situation. Learners put themselves into the shoes of someone experiencing an unknown and confusing situation to expand their tolerance of ambiguity. Learners no longer examine individual concepts in isolation; instead, they integrate cultural awareness, assumption checking, tolerance of ambiguity, and DAEA while navigating a realistic intercultural misunderstanding. This prepares learners for the remaining critical incidents, where they are expected to apply the same framework with increasing independence.

## Additional Critical Incidents
The scenarios were intentionally selected to represent different communication styles, social expectations, and cultural value systems rather than a single cultural region. This encourages learners to transfer the framework developed throughout the workshop to unfamiliar contexts rather than associating it with one specific culture. The scenarios represent a variety of cultural communication styles and value systems to encourage learners to apply the workshop concepts in unfamiliar contexts. The first one is a common “team building” practice in South Korea, Japan, and China where managers may take employees and business partners out drinking together. This is especially common in South Korea. The next one is a common practice is “face” cultures where directly saying “no” is considered rude. Instead people say it in an indirect way, like “that is difficult” to softly say no. This type of indirect communication can be unfamiliar to many people from communication styles that emphasize directness. The last one is a different view on community and family common in many Sub-Saharan African cultures.  
Unlike the Prague incident, these scenarios provide less instructional guidance, requiring learners to independently recognize assumptions, tolerate ambiguity, and apply DAEA to unfamiliar situations. This gradual reduction in scaffolding encourages learners to internalize the reflective process rather than relying on instructor guidance. This module is where learners get a chance to apply everything that has been covered so far.
These critical incidents, being broader and very different from each other, allow learners to practice all of the main learning outcomes. They get experience with other cultural perspectives by imagining they are in these situations, they practice tolerance of ambiguity by putting themselves in new and ambiguous situations, they then make plans for how they would handle situations like this in the future. These incidents provide repeated opportunities to apply the same reflective framework across culturally diverse contexts. The goal is for learners to recognize that while situations differ, the process for approaching them thoughtfully remains consistent.

## Final Reflection
The workshop concludes with a guided reflection that encourages learners to synthesize the concepts developed throughout the curriculum rather than viewing each module as an isolated lesson. Learners consider how their understanding of culture, assumptions, tolerance of ambiguity, and critical reflection has evolved and identify specific strategies they intend to apply in future intercultural interactions. Ending with reflection reinforces one of the central design principles of the workshop: learning is most valuable when it influences future behavior. We also give learners a final chance to review and update their personal definition of culture.

...

## Review
The review provides learners with an opportunity to revisit the workshop's core concepts, reinforce the relationships between them, and evaluate their own understanding before completing the workshop. Revisiting the concepts after completing the critical incidents helps strengthen long-term retention while emphasizing that the workshop's framework is designed to be used together rather than as a collection of independent techniques. Learners also get a chance to see what they have learned, how they have grown, and what they have accomplished in the course.

# Technical Implementation

## Accessibility
Accessibility was treated as a design requirement from the beginning of development rather than added as a final refinement. Because the workshop is designed for self-paced online learning, learners must be able to access both the instructional content and interactive activities regardless of how they navigate the site. Initial accessibility features include semantic HTML, logical heading structures, keyboard navigation, alternative text for all images, accessible color contrast, plain-text rendering of learner reflections, and print-friendly reflection summaries. Particular attention was given to complex instructional visuals, such as the Prague comic and DAEA diagrams, which include detailed alternative text so learners using screen readers receive equivalent instructional information. Additional accessibility improvements are planned as development continues. 

...

## Technical Design
Due to my background in both instructional design and prior web development experience, I intentionally used AI as a development partner rather than treating it as an automatic code generator. AI accelerated implementation, but architectural decisions, interaction design, instructional sequencing, accessibility requirements, and iterative refinements remained human-directed. Thus I worked with the help of AI to do a lot of the heavy lifting with code. One design idea I had was to limit the amount of HTML. If I made this the traditional way it would be many separate HTML files with thousands of lines of HTML in each. Thus I decided to have one page and have all content handled through JavaScript. This makes it a little bit more complicated to edit content, but much easier to locate it in each file. Additionally, borrowing from my background in Ruby on Rails, I decided to have separate JS files for the lessons and some of the reusable components that are used in different activities. Instead of copying JS from one module to another we have a separate JS file with many of the different activities. Two of the major components were a reusable guidedActivity component that shows up in many different modules as well as the reflectionSummary that touches on all modules. To tie it all together, and to show to learners as a type of assessment all reflections are saved locally in the browser. This allows learners to leave and return to the workshop without losing their work. Finally, the project is version controlled with Git and hosted through GitHub Pages. This makes ongoing revisions straightforward while keeping the instructional content synchronized with the repository.

## Reflection Summary
Reflections are a major part of this workshop series. Reflection was intentionally chosen because the workshop is designed to change how learners approach future intercultural situations rather than simply increasing factual knowledge. In person this workshop is done using discussions and a bit of socratic questioning. This isn’t really possible with online, remote learning. Thus, I settled on reflections. This way learners can reflect on their thoughts and feelings of the content, and then update their ideas after reflecting. This gives them a way to, in essence, have a conversation with themselves. Furthermore, when studying a topic like intercultural communication reflection is the best way of assessment, short of administering the Intercultural Developmental Inventory (IDI). Reflection, and more specifically critical reflection, also gives learners an opportunity to assess their own beliefs and plan for future action. This type of learning is also transferable to other fields, not only intercultural communication.

## Lessons Learned
The first biggest lesson learned was the possible workflows when collaborating with AI. This workshop started as a huge PowerPoint that slowly had to be converted into something that would work in web format. Activities had to be changed to account for the new format, and the lack of class interaction. AI was instrumental in converting the original PowerPoint activities and discussions into a web ready code. This came with a learning curve. AI is powerful but can also come up with a lot of ideas that can quickly become overdesigned. Another extremely valuable lesson was learning when not to follow AI suggestions. While AI generated many useful implementation ideas, it also tended to overcomplicate interactions or introduce unnecessary features. Maintaining a clear instructional vision became essential for deciding which suggestions improved the learning experience and which distracted from it. It takes finesse to work out the design detail without the AI coming up with new and different design changes. In the end though I got to a good and very good workflow. 
The next big lesson is reframing my thinking of lesson design from classroom to remote online. An added layer to this is not having a database, thus, no learner to learner interaction. Most of my teaching experience and education highlights the importance of interaction between learners, especially TESOL education. For this I had to design things differently. That’s why I settled on reflections. With most online courses you can still have interaction between learners by using forums and message boards, for this course I didn’t have that capability. Instead reflections are saved in the browser and accessible later. In essence, social reflection through discussion is replaced by individual reflection with the possibility for learners to return to previous reflections to see how their thinking has grown and changed.

## AI Collaboration
This project was developed through an iterative collaboration between instructional design expertise and AI-assisted software development. AI was used to accelerate implementation, prototype interactive components, generate reusable JavaScript structures, and assist with debugging and refactoring. However, instructional goals, curriculum sequencing, accessibility requirements, learner interactions, and final design decisions remained intentionally human-directed.
Rather than accepting AI-generated solutions automatically, each suggestion was evaluated against established learning outcomes and the project's Design Decisions document. This process frequently involved refining, simplifying, or rejecting AI-generated ideas when they did not support the intended learner experience. Maintaining a clear instructional vision proved essential to using AI effectively.
The project also demonstrates an evolving collaborative workflow. Early development involved separate planning and coding stages, while later development shifted toward a more integrated design-and-build process as AI development tools matured. This allowed instructional decisions and technical implementation to evolve together while maintaining consistency with the project's overall educational goals.

# Conclusion
The goal of this project was not simply to convert classroom materials into an online course.
It was to redesign those materials into a learning experience that encourages curiosity, critical reflection, and continued growth beyond the workshop itself.
Rather than ending when learners complete the final module, the workshop encourages them to continue practicing the competencies introduced throughout the course as they encounter new intercultural experiences.
